## The Best Ones

"Who gets to control the global thermostat?"
— Center for International Environmental Law (CIEL)

"Come on guys, this is getting embarrassing."
— Janos Pasztor, former UN Assistant Secretary-General on Climate Change, on Stardust's delays in publishing governance documents

"The way they're going about it is absolutely the most toxic, worst way."
— Daniele Visioni, Cornell University (refused to sign Stardust's NDA)

"There is nothing that makes people lose trust more than saying, 'Oh this is all secret.'"
— Daniele Visioni, Cornell University

"They claim to have a 'magic aerosol particle' but the assertion that it is 'perfectly safe and inert' cannot be trusted without published findings."
— Douglas MacMartin, Associate Professor, Cornell University

"They've ignored every recommendation from everyone and think they can turn a profit in this field."
— Douglas MacMartin, Cornell University

"Stardust Solutions and its investors are accelerating a reckless race that threatens to put humanity on a path of no return to perpetual dependency on this extreme technology."
— Center for International Environmental Law (CIEL)

"Even diamonds would alter stratospheric chemistry. Calling anything 'chemically inert' in the stratosphere is nonsense."
— David Keith, University of Chicago, founding director of Climate Systems Engineering Initiative (refused to consult for Stardust)

"When it comes to governance principles like transparency and public engagement — they're not adhering to any of them."
— Shuchi Talati, Alliance for Just Deliberation on Solar Geoengineering

"There's a massive incentive to lobby countries to use it, and that's the whole danger of having for-profit companies here."
— Shuchi Talati

"Adding business interests, profit motives, and rich investors into this situation just creates more cause for concern."
— David Keith & Daniele Visioni (MIT Technology Review)

"Don't mess with the sun's rays."
— EU Group of Chief Scientific Advisors (December 2024)

## The Contradictions

Chris Sacca previously stated he does "not think there should be private business models" in geoengineering — then his fund Lowercarbon Capital led the largest-ever private investment ($60M) in a for-profit geoengineering company.

Nebular founder Finn Murphy stated Stardust "can be worth tens of billions of dollars" — saying the quiet part loud about the profit motive.

Stardust CEO Yanai Yedvab: "We're not seeking funding or discussing specific laws or regulations — we're informing members of Congress about our work and the need for appropriate and robust oversight." — while simultaneously refusing to publish particle composition, refusing public consultation, and ignoring their own governance consultant's recommendations.
