# Make Sunsets — Comprehensive Profile

**Date:** 2026-03-19
**Topic:** Rogue geoengineering startup selling "cooling credits"

---

## Overview

Make Sunsets is a two-person company that releases sulfur dioxide (SO2) into the stratosphere via weather balloons and sells "cooling credits" to individuals and businesses. They represent the unregulated, move-fast-and-break-things end of geoengineering — doing what established researchers have only talked about, but without any scientific infrastructure, peer review, or governmental approval.

---

## Founding and Leadership

- **Founded:** October 2022
- **Founders:** Luke Iseman (CEO) and Andrew Song
- **Headquarters:** Box Elder, South Dakota (operations are run from an RV)
- **Employees:** Two. No scientists on staff.

### Luke Iseman

- **Education:** BSEcon from the Wharton School (University of Pennsylvania)
- **Previous roles:** Director of Hardware at Y Combinator; Partner Emeritus at YC
- **Inspiration:** Openly says he was inspired by Neal Stephenson's science fiction novel *Termination Shock* (2021), which depicts a billionaire unilaterally launching a geoengineering program
- Background is in hardware startups and venture capital, not atmospheric science

---

## What They Actually Do

- Release **sulfur dioxide (SO2)** via **biodegradable weather balloons** (not planes or drones)
- Balloons reach approximately **66,000 feet** (~20 km) — lower stratosphere
- **Total SO2 released to date:** approximately **0.1 tons** across all launches
- For context: a single coal plant emits thousands of tons of SO2 per year. Their releases are scientifically negligible.

### The "Cooling Credits" Model

- Sell credits at **$1-5 each**
- **900+ customers** have purchased credits
- **128,000+ credits sold** to date
- Core claim: **1 gram of SO2 offsets 1 ton of CO2 warming for 1 year**
- This claim is **not independently verified** and no peer-reviewed research supports their specific methodology

---

## Regulatory History

### Mexico (2022-2023)

- Conducted their **first launches in Baja California, Mexico**
- **Did not inform the Mexican government** before launching
- Mexico responded by **banning solar geoengineering** in January 2023 — the first country to do so
- Make Sunsets was effectively expelled from the country

### United States (2023-present)

- Relocated operations to **Reno, Nevada**, then to **South Dakota**
- **EPA** issued a **formal enforcement demand** under Clean Air Act Section 114 in **April 2025**
- **FAA claims scrutinized** — Make Sunsets implied they had airspace permissions near Reno, but the **Reno-Tahoe Airport Authority stated they gave no permissions**

## Scientific Credibility

- **No peer-reviewed research** of their own
- The science behind their claims is described as **"almost entirely modeling-based"**
- No measurement infrastructure to verify whether their releases have any detectable atmospheric effect
- At 0.1 tons total SO2, the releases are orders of magnitude below any threshold that would produce measurable cooling

### Criticism from Within Geoengineering

Even the established geoengineering research community condemns Make Sunsets:

- **David Keith** (Harvard, the leading SRM researcher) has called their approach reckless
- **Ken Caldeira** (Gates' climate advisor) views them as counterproductive to legitimate research
- The concern: Make Sunsets gives ammunition to people who want to ban all geoengineering research, making it harder for the institutional players to operate

---

## Key Concerns

1. **Selling something that doesn't work.** At their scale, the cooling credits are functionally meaningless — customers are paying for a symbolic gesture, not actual cooling.

2. **Regulatory evasion pattern.** Launched in Mexico without permission, got banned, moved to the US, faced EPA enforcement. The pattern is act first, deal with consequences later.

3. **No scientific accountability.** Two people with no atmospheric science background, no peer review, no measurement, no verification.

4. **Normalizing unilateral action.** If a two-person startup can release stratospheric aerosols from an RV, the precedent is set for anyone to do it.

---

## Key Takeaway

Make Sunsets occupies a strange position in the geoengineering landscape. Their actual environmental impact is essentially zero — 0.1 tons of SO2 is a rounding error. But their *political and regulatory* impact is significant. They forced Mexico into the first national geoengineering ban, triggered EPA enforcement action, and demonstrated that the governance gap is real: there is effectively nothing stopping someone from releasing aerosols into the stratosphere except a one-page Commerce Department notification filed 10 days in advance.

They are either reckless provocateurs or the canary in the coal mine showing what happens when technology outpaces governance. Possibly both.
